The Core Idea in Plain English

NGAME monitors the population of financial records — not just dollar amounts.

Every morning, NGAME asks QuickBooks: what records of each type existed yesterday, and what records exist today? For each of the 18 transaction categories it computes a score — the Churn Persistence Index (CPI) — measuring how stable the record population was overnight. During a 30-day training period NGAME learns what a normal day looks like for this specific organization. After training, every new day is compared against that baseline. A day that looks dramatically different — too many new vendors, a wave of deleted invoices, a sudden spike in bill payments — triggers an alert ranked by statistical severity.

Two-Phase Architecture

Phase I runs daily for 30 days and builds the statistical baseline. Phase II runs daily thereafter and compares today's behavior to that baseline. The Training Matrix is the bridge: it carries the learned mean (μ) and standard deviation (σ) for each transaction type forward into Phase II comparison.

The Monitoring Perimeter: 18 Transaction Types

NGAME extracts and monitors every transaction category QuickBooks recognizes. Each type receives its own CPI score each day, its own trained baseline, and its own independent z-score. The Greek letter φ (phi) is used to index types by position. φ18 — Vendors — carries special analytical weight because vendor manipulation is the most common vehicle for fraudulent disbursements.

Daily Data Foundation: The Snapshot Pair

Before NGAME computes anything each day, the Data Extraction Agent connects to QuickBooks via its secure read-only API and downloads a complete structured record of every active transaction of all 18 types. This is serialized into a machine-readable file called Today.ttl (the daily ontology snapshot). The previous day's snapshot is automatically renamed Yesterday.ttl.

Yesterday.ttl
The prior day's complete snapshot — every Customer record, Invoice, Bill, Vendor, etc., each identified by a unique record URI and carrying all its QuickBooks properties (amounts, dates, account codes, memos). This file is read-only once today's analysis begins.
Today.ttl
Today's freshly extracted snapshot from QuickBooks. Identical structure to Yesterday.ttl. The set-comparison between these two files is the computational engine of all NGAME analysis.

NGAME requires read-only API access to QuickBooks. It never writes to, modifies, or deletes any QBO record. Its role is strictly observational: extract, compare, and report.

The Core Measurement: CPI (Churn Persistence Index)

For each transaction type, NGAME compares the set of record identifiers present in Yesterday.ttl to those in Today.ttl. This produces three counts — the contingency partition:

Symbol Name Set Operation Plain-English Meaning
aRetainedYesterday ∩ TodayRecords present in both snapshots — survived overnight unchanged
bRemovedYesterday \ TodayRecords in yesterday but gone today — deleted or voided
cAddedToday \ YesterdayRecords in today but absent yesterday — newly created
nUniversea + b + cAll unique records seen across both days — the comparison universe

The CPI for transaction type k on day t is then the Jaccard similarity of the two record sets:

CPI_k = a / n = (Retained) / (Retained + Removed + Added) ∈ [0, 1]

CPI = 1.0

Every record persisted and nothing new appeared — perfect stability, zero churn. A healthy routine bookkeeping day for most transaction types.

CPI near 0

Almost no records survived and/or many new ones appeared — complete population turnover. Deeply anomalous for any type that should be stable.

CPI + α + δ = 1

CPI, Addition Rate (α = c/n), and Deletion Rate (δ = b/n) always sum to exactly 1. They partition the universe of records — a useful cross-check.

Why Jaccard similarity rather than simple count comparison?

Comparing record counts alone cannot distinguish between a day where 10 new vendors were created and 10 old ones deleted (count unchanged, but CPI is low — high churn and probable fraud) versus a stable day where 10 new vendors were legitimately onboarded and none deleted (count rises by 10, CPI stays near 1). Count-only analysis would miss the churn signal. CPI correctly weighs both new additions and deletions as signs of instability — detecting the pattern, not just the number.

Phase I: Training (Days 1–30)

The purpose of Phase I is to establish what normal looks like for this specific organization. A small nonprofit with stable vendor relationships has very different normal CPI patterns than a retail business with high invoice turnover. NGAME learns the organization's own baseline — not an industry average. This self-calibration is why the training period is essential.

The Training Matrix: Structure and Evolution

The Training Matrix is an Excel workbook managed entirely by NGAME. It starts with three fixed columns and grows by one CPI column per day. A second worksheet ("wCPI_Baseline") carries the same structure for the dollar-weighted CPI.

ColumnContentsWhen Updated
A — Transaction Type 18 rows: Customers (φ1) through Vendors (φ18) in fixed order Once, at initialization — never changes
B — μ (Mean CPI) Running mean CPI for each type across all training days recorded so far Recalculated each training day as a new column is added
C — σ (Std Dev CPI) Running standard deviation of CPI for each type Recalculated each training day alongside μ
D, E, F … (Day columns) Raw CPI value for each type on that specific training day One column appended per day; never overwritten

After 30 training days the matrix contains 33 columns: Transaction Type (A) + μ (B) + σ (C) + 30 daily CPI values (D through AG). The μ and σ columns in that final state constitute the trained baseline that Phase II compares against every subsequent day.

What μ and σ Mean in This Context

μ_k — Mean CPI for Transaction Type k

The average fraction of records of type k that persisted unchanged from one day to the next, across all training days. A Vendor μ of 0.995 means that on a typical day 99.5% of yesterday's vendor records still exist today — an extremely stable type. A Vendor μ of 0.85 means 15% of the vendor population turns over each day on average — already volatile, requiring a larger single-day deviation before NGAME classifies it as anomalous.

σ_k — Standard Deviation of CPI for Transaction Type k

How much the daily CPI for type k varies around its mean during training. A small σ means the organization's record-keeping for this type is very consistent — outliers stand out sharply even if they are small in absolute terms. A large σ means normal operations already include considerable day-to-day fluctuation — a larger deviation is needed before NGAME flags an anomaly. This is how NGAME self-calibrates to each organization without requiring an external benchmark.

Phase II: Analysis (Day 31 Onward)

Phase II runs every business day after training completes. The Data Extraction and CPI Analysis steps are identical to Phase I. What differs is what happens next: instead of appending to the Training Matrix, NGAME compares today's CPI array against the trained μ and σ to compute a z-score for each of the 18 transaction types, then ranks and escalates any anomalies through the rest of the pipeline.

Step 3: Z-Score Comparison

For each transaction type k, the Churn Comparison Agent loads μ_k and σ_k from the Training Matrix and computes the z-score:

z_k = ( CPI_k(today) − μ_k ) / σ_k

If σ_k = 0 (the type was perfectly stable on every training day — zero variation), z_k is set to 0 to avoid division by zero. The z-score is signed: a negative z means today's CPI is below normal (more churn than expected — a deletion-heavy or bulk-addition day); a positive z means today's CPI is above normal (less churn than expected — an unusually quiet day). Both directions are suspicious if extreme.

Alert thresholds apply to the absolute value |z_k| because both excessive stability and excessive instability relative to the baseline can signal manipulation. A day where every vendor was replaced is just as alarming as a day where none were, if neither pattern resembles the trained norm.

Alert Levels

Level Criterion Statistical Meaning Typical Action
HIGH |z_k| > 3.0 More than 3 standard deviations from the trained mean. A random day this extreme occurs fewer than 0.3% of the time under a normal distribution — a genuine statistical outlier. Immediate FRP review; escalate to management per engagement protocol
MEDIUM |z_k| > 2.0 More than 2 standard deviations from the trained mean. Affects roughly 5% of normal days — unusual but not extreme. Warrants investigation. Investigate and document; watch same type the following day
LOW |z_k| ≤ 2.0 Within the organization's normal range of variation. No action required; logged for completeness

Dollar-Weighted CPI (wCPI) — Secondary Signal

The standard CPI treats every record equally regardless of dollar amount — a $50,000 vendor payment counts the same as a $25 supply purchase. To make NGAME sensitive to disproportionate dollar values, a dollar-weighted variant (wCPI) is computed alongside the standard CPI. Instead of counting records, it weights each by its transaction dollar amount.

wCPI_k = (Dollar value of retained records) / (Dollar value of all records in the union)

wCPI much lower than CPI

New additions carry outsized dollar amounts relative to retained records. A single new vendor record representing a $40,000 payment barely moves the count-based CPI but sharply reduces the dollar-weighted wCPI. This compound signal surfaces high-value fraud that looks routine by record count alone.

wCPI much higher than CPI

Retained records dominate in dollar value while additions or deletions are high in count but small in amount — the organization's high-value records are stable, but there is churn in small-dollar records. Less alarming but still worth reviewing.

|wCPI − CPI| Divergence Dollar Signal Level Interpretation
> 0.30 HIGH New or removed records carry grossly disproportionate dollar value — a high-value transaction hiding behind count-level normalcy
> 0.15 MEDIUM Noticeable dollar imbalance; warrants examination of the specific records involved
≤ 0.15 LOW Dollar weighting consistent with record weighting — proportionate activity

Once sufficient wCPI training days have accumulated, the dollar signal shifts from a raw divergence threshold to a fully trained z-score (z_dollar = (wCPI − μ_w) / σ_w) using the same HIGH / MEDIUM / LOW bands. A composite alarm fires when EITHER the count-based OR the dollar signal reaches MEDIUM or HIGH — whichever is more sensitive on a given day.

Step 4: Anomaly Identification and Ranking

The Anomaly Identification Agent collects all 18 z-scores and ranks them from highest |z| to lowest. The Account Mapping Agent then resolves the φ index to specific QuickBooks account names and record identifiers, producing a human-readable anomaly list. A representative output might look like:

Rank Type (φ) CPI Today μ (Baseline) σ z-Score Level
1 φ18 — Vendors 0.720 0.995 0.005 −55.0 HIGH
2 φ6 — Bills 0.880 0.970 0.015 −6.0 HIGH
3 φ7 — Bill Payments 0.940 0.985 0.010 −4.5 HIGH
4 φ3 — Invoices 0.958 0.960 0.008 −0.3 LOW
5 φ1 — Customers 0.995 0.994 0.003 +0.3 LOW

The example above shows a shell-vendor fraud pattern: φ18 shows a massive CPI drop (many new vendors created, many old ones deleted simultaneously), accompanied by elevated Bill and Bill Payment activity — the classic vendor-manipulation signature where new vendors are created and immediately paid. This combination across three related types is more probative than any single type in isolation.

Step 5: LLM Analysis

The top-ranked anomalies are passed to the LLM Analysis Agent, which constructs a natural-language interpretation of each anomaly in the context of the organization's known transaction patterns.

Contextual Narrative
Translates z-score rankings into plain English: "Vendor type showed 28 new records created against a baseline of 0–2 per day, with 15 simultaneous deletions. This pattern is consistent with rapid shell-vendor insertion and suppression of audit trail."
Coordinated-Fraud Assessment
Evaluates whether anomalies across multiple types suggest a coordinated scheme (Vendor + Bill + Bill Payment anomalies co-occurring) versus an isolated data entry anomaly that affects only one type. Co-occurrence significantly increases the fraud probability assessment.
Recommended Actions
Generates specific follow-up steps calibrated to the detected pattern: "Review vendor creation logs for the 72-hour window, cross-reference with payment runs, verify authorization signatures on all new vendor approvals, confirm banking detail changes with source documents."

Step 6: Management Warning Report

The Management Warning Agent synthesizes all findings into a structured JSON report — NGAME's daily deliverable to the Fraud Risk Professional (FRP). The report includes: overall risk level (HIGH / MEDIUM / LOW), ranked anomaly list with supporting statistics, LLM narrative interpretation, and recommended actions.

The FRP receives this report each morning before any other action.

No alerts are sent automatically to management, the board, or the bookkeeper during initial deployment. The FRP reviews the report, exercises professional judgment, and decides what — if any — action to take. NGAME provides the statistical evidence; the FRP owns the response. This design preserves the engagement framework and avoids false-alarm fatigue.

Slow-Burn Detection: Multi-Day Sequence Analysis (SCPI)

A sophisticated perpetrator may spread fraudulent activity across many days — creating two or three shell vendors per day rather than thirty in one day. Each individual day produces only a MEDIUM alert; no single day crosses the HIGH threshold. Without sequence analysis this pattern evades detection.

Sequence amplification: |z_seq| ≈ |z_daily| × √L

When a transaction type consistently deviates from its training baseline by the same daily amount for L consecutive days, the sequence z-score (z_seq) grows by a factor of √L. A sustained MEDIUM deviation of −2σ per day over 5 days yields z_seq ≈ −2 × √5 ≈ −4.47 — solidly into HIGH territory. The √L amplification is the mathematical foundation for detecting slow-burn fraud that single-day analysis cannot see.

SCPI Formula

SCPI_k(t, L) = geometric mean of CPI_k over the last L days

= ( CPI_k(t) × CPI_k(t−1) × … × CPI_k(t−L+1) ) ^ (1/L)

z_seq_k = ( SCPI_k − μ_k ) / ( σ_k / √L )

The denominator σ_k / √L is the standard error of the geometric mean — it accounts for the fact that averaging over more days reduces random noise, making a sustained deviation more statistically decisive even when each individual day was only mildly anomalous. No retraining is required to enable sequence analysis; the same μ_k and σ_k from the Training Matrix are reused.

Property Mutation Detection

Beyond counting which records were added or deleted, NGAME also inspects every record that persisted from Yesterday to Today and checks whether any of its properties changed. This catches a distinct fraud class: modification of existing records rather than creation of new ones.

Mutation TypeExampleFraud Scenario
Added property A vendor record gains a new bank account number Unauthorized payment redirect — funds rerouted to a fraudster-controlled account
Removed property An invoice loses its "approved by" field Audit trail destruction — removal of authorization evidence before review
Modified property A bill's total amount changes from $1,200 to $12,000 Dollar amount inflation — escalating a legitimate bill to extract excess funds

Property mutations are reported separately from the CPI-based anomaly ranking and do not currently feed into the z-score vector. They are surfaced as a supplementary finding in the daily report for the FRP's review — especially valuable when the count-based CPI shows no anomaly but an existing record's critical fields have been altered.
