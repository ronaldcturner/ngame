The Detection Gap: Intended vs. Actual

Asset misappropriation via credit card depends on a single exploitable gap: the perpetrator controls both the card and the expense report. The bookkeeper codes the transaction how they're told — but the vendor name is a ground-truth record that cannot be falsified without also falsifying the bank statement.

NGAME's ontology preserves the vendor name independently from the GL category assigned to it. A vendor classified under Travel & Entertainment that maps to a cruise line, auto dealer, or casino is a structural anomaly — regardless of memo text.

Five Primary Detection Dimensions

1. Vendor-Category Mismatch (Highest Signal)

NGAME classifies each vendor name into its actual business type — independently of the GL account it was coded to. A charge to "Royal Caribbean" coded as "Travel" is technically travel-adjacent but should trigger a flag for personal luxury use, not business travel. A charge to "MGM Grand" coded as "Meals & Entertainment" is a casino.

2. Temporal Pattern Anomalies

Legitimate business purchases cluster around business hours and workdays. Personal use bleeds onto weekends, late evenings, and holidays — especially for recurring personal subscriptions (same vendor, same day each month).

Weekend / Holiday Charges
Business supply cards charged Friday night through Sunday for non-travel vendors. Compare against employee PTO and travel calendar.
Monthly Recurring Personal
Same vendor, same amount, same day of month. Streaming services, gym memberships, auto loan payments — recurring personal billing is a fingerprint.
Off-Cycle Spikes
Large charges to personal-category vendors just before month-end close, before audits, or after approval chain changes — exploiting timing gaps.

3. Structuring / Threshold Evasion

Many small organizations have informal approval thresholds — charges below $25, $50, or $100 may not require a receipt or manager approval. Perpetrators often split large personal purchases into multiple smaller charges, or use a vendor that processes naturally small amounts.

Pattern Description Detection Method
Clustering just below threshold Multiple charges at $24.xx, $49.xx, $99.xx to same vendor Amount distribution clustering analysis
Same-vendor splits on same day Two or more charges to same merchant on same day Same-day duplicate vendor detection
Gradual escalation Personal vendor charges growing slowly over months Vendor spend trend analysis
Small round-number charges $20.00, $50.00 to cash-like or ambiguous vendors Round-amount flag on personal-category vendors

4. Card-Purpose Deviation

Many organizations issue cards with a stated purpose: one card for travel, one for supplies, one for fuel. NGAME can model the expected vendor-category distribution for each card and flag charges that fall outside that distribution.

In QBO this is representable through Payment Method tagging. If each card is a distinct payment method, NGAME can build a per-card vendor-category profile and flag drift — a fuel card used at a casino, or a supplies card at a cruise line.

5. Missing Audit Trail Indicators

In QBO, charges can carry attachments (receipts), memos, and reconciliation flags. Personal charges consistently lack receipts or carry vague memos ("supplies", "misc") — while charges to legitimate vendors tend to have documentation. This documentation gap is itself a signal.

Indicator Personal Use Signal
No attachment on large charge Deliberate — a cruise receipt or casino statement would be self-incriminating
Generic memo text "Misc", "Supplies", "Expense" on vendors that should have specifics
Charge never reconciled Perpetrator avoids drawing attention during bank reconciliation review
Charge split to obscure vendor Line-item splits that bury the vendor name inside a multi-line entry

Honest Limitations

NGAME operates on what QBO records. Several signals are absent or weak:

Limitation Why It Matters Potential Mitigation
No MCC codes in QBO QBO does not import Merchant Category Codes from the card network — only the vendor name as the processor labeled it NGAME builds its own vendor taxonomy from vendor names; can be augmented with bank feed enrichment if available
Vendor name quality varies Some card processors truncate or mangle names ("SQ *", "TST *", "AMZN MKTP") Pattern matching and normalization layer; flag ambiguous vendor names for human review
No cardholder identity in transaction QBO CC charges don't record which employee made the charge — only which card Card-to-employee mapping must be maintained separately and joined at analysis time
Creative GL coding A sophisticated perpetrator may reclassify charges to plausible categories (real vendor, wrong category) Temporal and amount patterns still surface; vendor type is harder to fake than category
